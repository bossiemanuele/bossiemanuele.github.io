## Overview

This project investigates the use of **causality-informed machine learning** to improve prediction of the North American Monsoon system. The focus is on identifying statistically significant relationships between atmospheric variables and precipitation patterns, with particular attention to **time-lagged dependencies**.

Understanding these relationships is critical for improving predictive models in climate science, where delayed interactions between variables often play a key role in system dynamics.

## My Contribution

I contributed to this research effort by focusing on the **information-theoretic analysis of lagged dependencies** between atmospheric variables and rainfall.

Specifically, my work involved:

- Computing **mutual information (MI)** between selected atmospheric variables and precipitation data  
- Analyzing **time-lagged relationships** to identify delayed dependencies  
- Evaluating how information transfer varies across different temporal offsets  
- Supporting the identification of variables with potential **causal influence** on rainfall patterns  

This role was focused on the **data analysis and feature discovery stage** of the broader project, providing insights to guide downstream modeling and prediction efforts.

## Technical Approach

The analysis is based on computing mutual information between variables \(X\) (atmospheric signals) and \(Y\) (precipitation), while introducing a time lag \(\tau\):

\[
I(X_t ; Y_{t+\tau})
\]

By sweeping across different values of \(\tau\), the framework identifies:
- **Leading indicators** of precipitation  
- Time delays at which variables carry the most predictive information  
- Nonlinear dependencies that may not be captured by standard correlation analysis  

## Impact

This work contributes to the development of **causality-informed predictive models** for climate systems by:

- Identifying relevant variables and time scales for monsoon prediction  
- Supporting more interpretable machine learning models  
- Enhing understanding of delayed interactions in atmospheric dynamics  

The methodology is broadly applicable to other geophysical and environmental systems where **lagged, nonlinear relationships** are central to system behavior.
