## Overview

This project focused on the design of a **human-in-the-loop experimental framework** to study **trust in AI systems** within aviation safety-critical environments.

As AI systems become increasingly integrated into cockpit decision-making and air traffic operations, understanding how humans **trust, rely on, and interact with AI** is essential. Miscalibrated trust—either over-reliance or under-utilization—can lead to degraded performance and safety risks.

## Research Objective

The goal of this project was to investigate:

> **How do transparency, reliability, and control influence human trust and decision-making when interacting with AI systems in aviation contexts?**

The study aimed to quantify how these factors affect:
- Decision accuracy  
- Response time  
- Reliance on AI recommendations  

## Experimental Design

A controlled experimental framework was developed to simulate **AI-assisted decision-making in aviation scenarios**.

### Key Components

- **Simulated Environment**  
  Participants would interact with a flight-related decision-making interface where an AI system provides recommendations.

- **AI Assistance Module**  
  The AI would generate suggestions (e.g., classifications, alerts, or decisions), with controlled variations in:
  - Accuracy (error rates)  
  - Transparency (explainability of outputs)  
  - Level of autonomy  

- **Human Interaction**  
  Participants would choose whether to follow or override AI recommendations.

## Variables and Measurements

The framework was designed to measure both **behavioral** and **subjective** aspects of trust:

### Independent Variables
- AI reliability (low vs. high accuracy)  
- Transparency (black-box vs. explainable outputs)  
- Level of human control  

### Dependent Variables
- **Decision accuracy**  
- **Reaction time**  
- **Rate of AI reliance / override behavior**  
- **Self-reported trust levels** (via post-task surveys)  

## Status

While the experimental framework and study design were fully developed, **data collection and experimental validation were not completed** within the project timeline.

Nevertheless, the project established a **foundation for future human-subject experimentation**, including:
- Clearly defined hypotheses  
- Reproducible experimental structure  
- Measurable trust and performance metrics  

## Impact

This work contributes to the broader field of **human-AI teaming** by addressing a critical challenge:

> Designing AI systems that are not only accurate, but also **appropriately trusted**.

The proposed framework is applicable to:
- Aviation decision-support systems  
- Autonomous and semi-autonomous vehicles  
- Safety-critical human-machine interfaces
